Navigating the OBBBA Tax Changes

This guide is designed for tax professionals to quickly understand and apply the significant tax law changes introduced by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA). Use this resource with client scenarios to advise your individual and business clients effectively.

High-Level Tax Impact

Individual Tax Changes

Rates & Standard Deduction

OBBBA makes the individual tax rates and higher standard deduction from the TCJA permanent, preventing the scheduled "tax cliff" in 2026. The standard deduction is also increased beyond the TCJA levels.

State and Local Tax (SALT) Deduction

The bill temporarily increases the cap on the SALT deduction for certain taxpayers before reverting to the old limit.

Tax YearOld Law CapNew Law Cap (OBBBA)
2025$10,000$40,000
2026-2029$10,000Increases annually by 1%
2030+$10,000$10,000

Important: MAGI Phase-Down

For tax years 2025-2029, the increased cap is reduced for higher-income taxpayers. The cap is reduced by 30% of the amount by which a taxpayer's MAGI exceeds the threshold, but the cap will not be reduced below $10,000.

  • 2025 MAGI Threshold: $500,000 (for all filers except MFS)
  • Married Filing Separately: All caps and thresholds are halved.

Client Scenario: A married couple in a high-tax state has a MAGI of $600,000 and paid $50,000 in property and state income taxes in 2025.

  • Excess MAGI: $600,000 - $500,000 = $100,000
  • Cap Reduction: $100,000 * 30% = $30,000
  • New SALT Cap: $40,000 (initial cap) - $30,000 (reduction) = $10,000
  • Conclusion: Despite the higher cap, their income is too high, and their SALT deduction remains capped at $10,000.

Family & Dependent Credits

OBBBA makes significant, permanent enhancements to key tax provisions for families with children and dependents.

Client Scenario: A client with two children under 13 pays $16,000 in daycare expenses. Their AGI is $120,000.

  • Option 1 (Child and Dependent Care Credit): The credit is 50% of eligible expenses, up to $8,000. The credit phases down above $125k AGI. At $120k AGI, they get the full 50% credit. Credit = 50% * $8,000 = $4,000 tax savings.
  • Option 2 (Dependent Care FSA): They can contribute $7,500 pre-tax. Assuming a 22% marginal federal rate, the savings are $7,500 * 22% = $1,650 tax savings (plus state tax savings).
  • Conclusion: For this client, the enhanced tax credit provides a significantly larger benefit than the FSA.

New Targeted Deductions (Temporary)

The bill introduces three high-profile, temporary deductions aimed at middle-class taxpayers. These expire after 2028.

Business Tax Changes

Pass-Throughs & Corporations

OBBBA provides permanent tax relief and certainty for both pass-through entities and C-corporations.

  • Sec. 199A QBI Deduction: The 20% deduction for Qualified Business Income from pass-throughs is made permanent.
  • Corporate Tax Rate: The 21% flat corporate tax rate is made permanent.

Practice Point: The permanence of the QBI deduction solidifies its importance in choice-of-entity analysis. Continue to evaluate S-Corp vs. C-Corp structures for clients, now with the certainty that these key provisions will remain.

Capital Investment & Expensing

The bill makes permanent two of the most powerful pro-growth provisions for business investment.

  • 100% Bonus Depreciation: The ability to immediately deduct 100% of the cost of eligible property is made permanent, reversing the scheduled phase-down.
  • R&D Expensing: The requirement to amortize R&D expenses over 5 years is repealed. Businesses can once again immediately expense domestic R&D costs.

Information Reporting (Form 1099)

The threshold for issuing Form 1099-MISC and 1099-NEC for payments to non-employees is significantly increased.

LawReporting Threshold
Old Law$600
New Law (OBBBA)$2,000 (indexed for inflation)

Client Scenario: Your small business client paid an independent contractor $1,800 for services in tax year 2026.

  • Under Old Law: A Form 1099-NEC would be required since the payment is over $600.
  • Under New Law: No Form 1099-NEC is required since the payment is below the new $2,000 threshold.
  • Conclusion: This change reduces the administrative burden for clients making many small payments to contractors.
